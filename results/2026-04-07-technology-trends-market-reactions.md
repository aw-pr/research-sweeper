## Summary

The six months from October 2025 to April 2026 were defined by massive AI infrastructure investment, two major market shocks (DeepSeek and tariffs), a quantum computing speculative bust, and the beginning of structural AI-driven demand shifts in energy, healthcare, and finance. Technology stocks as a class defied repeated challenges to maintain elevated valuations — but warning signs on concentration and valuation are mounting.

## 1. The DeepSeek Shock (27 January 2025) — and Its Long Aftermath

**The event:** Chinese startup DeepSeek released R1 on January 20, 2025, claiming it trained a ChatGPT-competitive model for just $5.6 million in compute — a fraction of US competitors' costs. The market reaction was immediate and violent:

- **NVIDIA fell 17%**, losing **$589 billion in market capitalisation** — the largest single-day market cap loss in stock market history
- Nasdaq dropped 3.1%; S&P 500 fell 1.5%
- ASML, TSMC, Broadcom all fell sharply

**The recovery:** Within 24 hours, Nvidia rebounded 8.9%. By October 2025, Nvidia hit a **$5 trillion market capitalisation** — the first company ever to reach that level. Full-year 2025 return: **+39%**.

**The longer view:** By January 2026, one year on, the companies most affected had not just recovered but continued to grow. The underlying demand story was unchanged: Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are now expected to deploy **~$475 billion in combined capex in 2026**. DeepSeek's efficiency gains appear to have expanded total AI demand rather than replacing it. DeepSeek's next model (V4) will run on Huawei chips rather than Nvidia, and Chinese model consumption on OpenRouter surpassed US models in February 2026 — a geopolitical signal rather than a demand collapse.

## 2. The April 2025 Tariff Shock and Recovery

**The event:** On April 2, 2025 ("Liberation Day"), President Trump announced sweeping tariffs. The S&P 500 fell nearly 5% on April 3 (worst session since March 2020); Nasdaq fell 6%. Apple — with ~80% of iPhone manufacturing in China and ~20% of sales — dropped more than 9%. Semiconductor stocks (Marvell, Broadcom, Lam Research) fell 10%+ each; Micron sank >16%.

**Recovery:** On April 9, the administration announced a tariff pause; markets posted their largest single-day gains in years. By June 27, 2025, both the S&P 500 and Nasdaq closed at all-time highs.

**Full-year 2025 results:**
- S&P 500: **+17.9%** (total return basis; third straight year of double-digit gains)
- Nasdaq Composite: **+21.2%**
- PHLX Semiconductor Index (SOX): **+35.9%**
- Nvidia: **+39%**
- Broadcom: **+50%+**

**Ongoing tariff risk in 2026:** Trump's 15% global tariff announced in 2025 continues to threaten semiconductor supply chains. Apple committed an additional $100 billion to US companies/suppliers over four years to seek tariff exemption. AMD faces cost pressure from TSMC's manufacturing tariff exposure.

## 4. Quantum Computing: The Speculative Boom-Bust-Boom Cycle

Quantum computing stocks experienced two separate speculative manias and crashes in a single 12-month window:

**December 2024 – January 2025 (Boom 1):** Pure-play stocks IonQ, Rigetti, D-Wave Quantum, and Quantum Computing Inc. rose by up to **5,400%** on a trailing 12-month basis at peak. Driven by Google's Willow chip demonstration (below-threshold quantum error suppression, December 2024) and Microsoft's Majorana 1 chip (topological qubits, February 2025).

**January–September 2025 (Crash and recovery):** Stocks crashed immediately after entering 2025, slowly recovered, then **crashed again around October 2025**. IonQ and peers fell 10–15% from December peaks into early 2026.

**Key performance numbers:**
- Rigetti: Up ~4,000%+ from prior year at peak; ~$32/share by late 2025
- IonQ: Surged to ~$70 (170%+ in six months); $22 billion market cap (Oct 2025)
- D-Wave: Up ~458% trailing year at peak

**The problem:** There is almost no evidence businesses are generating a positive return on quantum investments. IonQ became the first quantum company to cross $100 million GAAP revenue ($130 million at 202% YoY), but valuations are wildly ahead of commercial reality. The narrative has shifted from physical qubit counts to logical qubits and error correction — still years from practical advantage.

**Major tech plays:** Alphabet, IBM, and Microsoft are the safer bets — they have quantum programmes but are not dependent on them. IBM's Nighthawk processor (360 qubits, 2026) is designed to demonstrate "quantum advantage."

AI data centre electricity demand has made nuclear energy one of the hottest sectors in the market. US data centre electricity demand is expected to climb from 19 GW (2023) to 35 GW (2030); Goldman Sachs estimates a 160% increase by 2030.

## 7. AI Impacts on Non-Tech Industries

AI has moved beyond tech into core operational adoption across sectors:

**Healthcare:** 94% of healthcare firms now view AI as a core operational requirement; AI healthcare market projected to reach $431 billion by 2032 (36.83% CAGR). Key use cases: diagnostic image analysis (radiology), administrative automation, drug discovery acceleration.

**Finance:** AI adoption among CFOs more than doubled in one year to **72%**. Primary applications: forecasting/planning (81%), risk management (74%), treasury management (68%).

**Agriculture:** 80% AI adoption rate. Precision agriculture with sensor/satellite/drone analysis — soil health, moisture, crop growth, irrigation optimisation.

**Manufacturing:** PepsiCo + Siemens + NVIDIA deploying 3D digital twins (AI agents identify 90% of issues before physical modification; 20% throughput increase, 10–15% capex reduction). Telcos had the highest agentic AI adoption rate at 48%.

**General:** 88% of companies use AI in at least one business function (up from 78% one year prior); companies report 3.7× ROI per dollar invested in generative AI (PwC 2026 AI Predictions).

## 11. Valuation Warning Signs and AI Bubble Debate

The AI enthusiasm has driven meaningful concentration risk:

- **CAPE ratio ~37** (November 2025) — top 10% since 1988; approaching dot-com levels
- The five largest companies represent **30% of the S&P 500** and **20% of MSCI World** — greatest concentration in half a century
- Capital Economics (Chief Markets Economist John Higgins): "The AI stock bubble has already burst" (March 2026)
- One AI bubble has burst (application layer, speculative AI stocks), but Fortune (March 2026) notes a second, rarer type of bubble — the infrastructure/hyperscaler spending bubble — may still be growing
- Sam Altman stated in 2025 that he believes an AI bubble is ongoing
- Goldman Sachs expects the S&P 500 to rally ~12% in 2026; Bank of America projects only ~3.7% gain

**Source quality note:** Bubble assessments are opinion-based; the underlying financial data (CAPE, concentration ratios) from RBC Wealth Management and Vanguard research is more authoritative than analyst forecasts.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Both shocks (DeepSeek, Tariffs) were absorbed** — markets recovered to all-time highs within weeks/months. The AI investment thesis has so far proven durable against repeated challenges.
2. **Hyperscaler capex is the single most important market signal** — $600–700 billion committed for 2026 underwrites the semiconductor sector. This is the primary reason Nvidia's correction from $207 to ~$177 has not spooked the long-term bulls.
3. **Quantum computing remains speculative** — the boom-bust pattern will likely repeat. Real value is in large tech companies with quantum programmes as optionality, not in pure-play stocks priced for breakthroughs that remain years away.
4. **Nuclear energy is the most structurally changed non-tech sector** — AI demand has directly catalysed multi-decade power purchase agreements, restarting retired plants and funding new reactor development.
5. **AI bubble concern is legitimate but not imminent consensus** — concentration at historic highs, CAPE near dot-com levels. The risk is real; the timing is uncertain.
6. **Non-tech sectors are adopting AI at operating, not experimental, levels** — healthcare, finance, and manufacturing are reporting concrete ROI, not just pilots.
7. **China's AI ecosystem** is growing in independence (Huawei chips for DeepSeek) and reach (OpenRouter consumption), creating a structural US-China AI decoupling that will increasingly affect hardware export policy and market structure.
